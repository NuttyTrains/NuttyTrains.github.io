You know what makes the best NT adventures? Having Matthew so stressed and so ded that he has had multiple mental breakdowns and is fully committed to drive the adventure into the ground, and drive us both off a cliff! There have been multiple masterpiece adventures in the past, dictated by Matthew's shattered consciousness, but none had ever come close to this level of insanity. None had pushed the limits of what was possible quite so far before. Never before had we come so close to the brink. That was all about to change. Welcome to Koppa 11, the 100-station adventure.

Amusingly, this was meant to be a fairly gentle adventure. James was in Guildford for a PhD interview and we were going to have a slow amble around Surrey, ticking off some quiet commuter towns and some little branchlines, staying well away from London. Oh boy were we wrong.

We met in the car park outside the University of Surrey in Guildford, Matthew arriving a few minutes late after getting distracted by Fluids 2. James gave a whistle-stop tour, Matthew received his 3rd coffee of the morning, the cameras came out, and we were off. Off very quickly, I should add. We were late for the Alton train and so absolutely legged it to the station. Through the car park, down the street, into the station, along the bridge, down the stairs and onto the train, all in no time at all. Got to start your day properly - the NT way.

Things went pretty much as planned though. We wound our way towards Farnborough, through quiet fields, rolling countryside and endless dreary suburbia, changed at Farnham, and ended up at the end of the line in Alton, in the middle of nowhere in Hampshire. The station was attached to a heritage railway station, which was cool. We had a brief look outside the station. James saw a Range Rover and a Waitrose, gave it his seal of approval, and no sooner than that we were back on the train.

This time we took the other line out of Farnham, joining the mainline to Woking. We talked a bit about GM and Darryl (whom Matthew would soon meet and be unable to avoid), before pulling into Woking in no time at all. This was what we had been waiting for. The main destination of this adventure. The highlight. Pizza Express in Woking.

We had a quick wander around the town, seeing a War of the Worlds statue and nothing much else (it was a bit shit), before heading to the Pizza Express, conveniently situated near a daycare centre.

As we were both over 18, we weren't able to leave Pizza Express in a classy Range Rover, so we walked back to the station instead. We got more caffeine for Matthew, and got on the train once more, heading into London, up the mainline, ticking off plenty of crappy suburban stations along the way. This part of the country is grim. Why these stations (in such expensive areas) are so soul-destroyingly depressing, I will never know. All I know is that we were glad to leave Byfleet, Weybridge and Walton behind, and we soon were in Surbiton. We changed to the Hampton Court branch line and ticked off the 2 stations there, before getting off to have a look around.

We had only a few minutes, so we left the station and started walking towards the Thames. Fortunately, Matthew's mental breakdown was passed, so this was not to throw himself into the river, but to go down to the pier and get a good shot of the palace on the other bank. Here we filmed an excellent clip of James giving a history lesson - like the TV show Drunk History, except (this time) sober. Still filled with beautiful inaccuracies though. Watch out 2024 - this one's going viral!

We then crossed the bridge to the other side, finally entering London and causing James to have imminent lung failure/bronchitis. We filmed the last bit of "Sober" "History", noticed the time, and sprinted back into the safety of the fresher air of Surrey, through the ticket barriers and back onto the train, completely missing the terminus sign. Alas, it was meeting time for James, and it had been approximately 3 hours since Matthew had done fluids, and 0.1 nanoseconds since he thought about them, so we now began our next leg of the journey, towards the brand-new (to NT) Sutton Premier Inn, conveniently and not at all dodgily located around the back of the shopping centre, meant to the money-laundering operation and the coke dealer.

This was easier said than done. The railway lines were not in our favour, and Matthew's desire to choose a horribly contorted route that would make a topologist scream didn't help. We returned to Surbiton and continued up the line further towards Clapham Junction. The intention was to change and Wimbledon and take Thameslink to Sutton. Nice and simple. Matthew had other ideas though. As we approached Wimbledon, Matthew started to consult the official NT Orphan map (T-shirt available in children sizes soon), and proposed a nutty idea - why not go one stop further to Earlsfield, make a 0 minute connection, and double back on ourselves to tick off that last station on the line? This was clearly a terrible idea. It was perfect.

The train pulled up, came to a halt, and no sooner had the doors opened did we sprint out, armed with James' dynamically destabilising bag. Down the stairs, along the corridor, through the barriers, through the other barriers, up the stairs, onto the platform, and onto the waiting train, all in less than 60 seconds. Truly the NT way.

We returned to Wimbledon and disembarked this time, as we were headed to our actual destination this time. To Thameslink! Or not? Yet again, Matthew was scheming. Why not travel via yet another branch line and tick off even more stations? What could go wrong? We waited for the Chessington train, as the clock ticked down. The Chessington train was kill, so we caught some other train 1 stop, changed yet again and headed to Epsom instead. We reversed directions again at Epsom (see what I mean about Brownian motion), and finally went to Sutton with only a few minutes to spare.

He definitely wouldn't allow Matthew to drag him down yet another branch line. Yet that is exactly what we did. It was off to Hayes.

We really, really should have gone home now. It was gone half 9, and we were exactly 50 stations in. Yet for some Godforsaken reason, Matthew was plotting and planning again, and for some even more Godforsaken reason, James was willing to go along with this insanity. It was up to Lewisham, where, in the pissing rain, we stood on the platform, with Matthew calculating just how many ridiculous and improbable changes could be made and how far we could go before the clock ran out, and we were abandoned somewhere in South East London for the night. We caught the first train to Dartford, leaving London yet again, this time visiting Kent for the first time on NT.

Matthew had had the entire remainder of the complex system of changes planned by this point, but out of nowhere, a late Thameslink train to Slade Green showed up. To hell with the plan. We were making this -4 minute connection. It was sprinting time yet again, at half 10. We jumped on the train, and switched yet again at Slade Green. God, if people could see our full journey history, they'd think we were crackheads or AIs. We passed through Abbey Wood (the Elizabeth line terminus - foreshadowing!) and alongside the line to Woolwich. We ticked off both Woolwich Arsenal and Greenwich from the DLR, to complete the 2nd line to Dartford along an improbable and time-saving combination of trains.

We leapt off the train at Deptford, and wandered down the muggily-lit Deptford High St, turned into a completely dim park, and got back on the train at New Cross. All that we needed to do now was get on the Slowverground, head straight to Clapham Junction, and head home. But no. Not on NT. Not when we were merely 13 hours in. Not with only 90 stations ticked off. Not at 11pm. We could do moar. 1 more ridiculous journey to cap things off. We went up to Canada Water, unifying 2 sections of the slowverground on the Official NT ¬Orphan map, sprinted through the station, along the platform, down the stairs, through the atrium, in and out of the passengers, up the escalators, and onto the other platform for the reverse train to take us to Clapham Junction.

OOH JAMIE, NO GOING TO PECKHAM! NO, NO, NOT A NICE PLACE. NO. SAY HI TO DEL BOY AND MAAAATHEWWW!

Through Peckham Rye, Denmark Hill and Clapham High Street we went, reaching the golden milestone of 100 stations in 1 adventure. Never before, never again. 81 NR stations and 19 tram stations. We did more stations on this one day than we had done in entire 12 month periods in the past. And yet, we were still somehow alive. Was this true insanity? Had we finally reached the brink? [Future Matthew: Oh Hell no. It gets much worse!].

We pulled into Clapham Junction, walked for what seemed like a mile to James' platform, and parted ways after a very long day, both just about making it home before the trains finally stopped for the night. It isn't often that we have an adventure that is immediately one of the greats, but this was the exception. Koppa 11 was instantly iconic, and one of the best adventures we have ever had. It truly put the Nutty into Nutty Trains.
