A trip to London 1 week before exams purely because James scammed some arts student? What a great idea! Cue a Chinatown extravaganza and multiple failed attempts to reach the edge of West London, all whilst the tube falls apart around us!

James said to Matthew on his birthday, "Are you free tomorrow? I fancy scamming some art student out of their train tickets for a fiver each way". Matthew said he'd be up for an adventure, and thus the stage was set! James hopped on the train at 6:30 in the morning, and he was off to PAD!

He arrived in PAD 6 hours later, where he met Matthew leaving the circle-district, and we both barrelled down to the Bakerloo line to head to Chinatown. The rickety ancient train screamed and rumbled and shook its way down the line as we sat there misanthropically like true Londoners. We got off at Piccadilly Circus, where Matthew did some quick off-the-top-of-his-head calculations as to where on earth we were, quickly worked out which direction North was (to James's great amusement, as in doing so, Matthew pointed in more directions than a weathervane), and we set off, passing TfL's new Orwellian art installation, and exited into Piccadilly Circus, before walking the short distance to Chinatown, which James had been looking forward to immensely. We rounded the corner, and were greeted by the magnificent Chinatown gate, which James had his picture in front of:

James adored Chinatown. After a year of being indoctrinated studying the words of our glorious leader Xi Jing Ping, James was so excited to actually finally, be surrounded by people speaking the language he had spent the best part of a year studying. They were not unimpressed by the amount of Chinese. IT WAS EVERYWHERE. China had definately got it's grip on this area of London. All the restuarants had 烤鸭 spinning in the windows and there was Chinese supermarkets, selling a wide range of proudcts from the glorious homeland, there was definately no Cantonese and definitely nothing to do with Hong Kong as that makes Big Daddy 西, very very unhappy. It was absolutely packed everywhere, it had Chinese street signs, a plethora of authentic Chinese restaurants and Chinese lanterns strung up in every street - even the back alley's bins had Chinese on them! We walked through the entirety of the enclave, before wandering up and down Gerrard Street, looking for a nice place to eat. We settled on Four Seasons, and were quickly shown to an outside table, as indoor dining was not yet allowed. Matthew ordered some mixed seafood fried noodles and some vegetable spring rolls, and James ordered 宫保鸡丁, 米饭, and 茶... in Chinese! The food was excellent, and we spent ages talking about China. The tea was delicious - some kind of green lotus Jasmine tea (and their chopsticks were excellent too).

After paying, we went to 2 different Chinese supermarkets on Gerrard Street, where James bought some udon 面 and some 清涛啤酒 for his hash the next day. Then we walked to Covent Garden, where he bought some cards to do a Faroh shuffle with in his upcoming magic show (also the next day), and had a wonderful conversation with the stall-holder, which began as follows:

Stallholder: So are you an amateur magician?
James: No, no, I am a mathemagician!

It turns out that the stall-holder learned more from that conversation than James did himself! After this, we walked to Holborn, narrowly avoiding the enormous anti-lockdown protest, getting slightly lost along the way, passing some Kings College London buildings and walking along Drury Lane, before getting on the tube to begin the second half of our adventure! Our original plan had been to try and attack at least 3 of the western branches of the tube: Heathrow, Uxbridge, and Amersham/Chesham. In Matthew's head, where time means very little at all, this seemed like a fantastic idea and perfectly doable in the time we had left. But this was Matthew time, so as we would find out, this would be impossible.

We just missed the Piccadilly line train, and so we waited a few minutes and hopped on the next one to Rayners Lane. Almost immediately, it became apparent that our original plan would be too time-consuming - the map in the station indicated that it was an hour to both Heathrow and Uxbridge. Yikes. This was much more than Matthew had thought. Therefore, we abandoned our original Plan A of going to Heathrow, and instead decided to go straight to Uxbridge, with the hope of returning to London via the Met. Before too long at all, the train pulled into the station, we jumped on, and it departed, screaming its way through the tunnels, like a pacer with a megaphone, and rattled and shook the whole way. We had already done the first few stations, so once again, we sat there misanthropically. Then, when we reached Hyde Park Corner, we started jumping on and off - NT had begun!

Leading on from Koppa 3, we decided to film little clips of each station, so out came the phone camera, to catch random out-of-context clips of station signs and screaming trains, whilst we played the hokey cokey with the train doors, jumping back and forth, in and out, and switching carriages over and over, to the confusion and annoyance of the other passengers. Soon, we arrived at South Kensington station, which was closed until 2022, and we saw the supremely strange sight of the deserted platform, with a 5mph sign in bright yellow, and "STATION CLOSED" on all the roundels - very odd. What was most odd was that all the lights were still on, and that the train slowed down to almost stopping speed - what was the point of that?

We passed through Matthew's home station, Gloucester Road, and before long we were above ground (which James preferred), and were zipping through leafy West London. After Hammersmith we reached the long sprint section of the Piccadilly, where Matthew has previously zipped past and overtaken 3 district trains in one journey. This time however, we were disappointed - no district line trains to be seen, and indeed, we were overtaken by the train behind us at Acton Town, to Matthew's dismay and astonishment. We then headed up to North Ealing, where we decided to wait until the next Uxbridge train, as North Ealing is a nice quaint station.

This was soon to be a mistake. We got off, and found an empty dot matrix (a very rare sight), and Matthew became suspicious that something was up. We decided to kill time by exploring the station. Initially, we wandered up and down the northbound platform remarking at how quaint the station was (it helped that it was a beautiful, quiet day). We crossed over the little wooden bridge to the other platform, and saw the wood-panelled tube roundels. We walked to the very end of the platform, and Matthew began to measure the length of the entire platform with his feet, just like Vicki in All The Stations.

Shortly after, we were greeted with the announcement "Due to a customer incident, there will be no trains for 35 minutes". Fuck. Why are the trains always, always fucked whenever James comes to London? Determined to kill time, Matthew killed time by continuing to measure the platform with his feet (roughly a foot long), to the confusion and amusement of the increasingly busy station, as James filmed the entire thing. Finally, he finished - it was 311 Matthew feet long!

James: This one's for the foot fetishists!

We crossed back over the bridge, investigated the rare free station toilets (well, free for women - men have to cross the ticket line if they want to use them), and then left the station in search of Plan C - West Acton. We walked through Hanger Hill, which was very beautiful (and James greatly approved - he had a new favourite area!). The entire place is interwar, mock-Tudor housing, and it is right next to Ealing and 2 tube stations, and has a very calm and quiet air to it.

Plan C involved doubling back on ourselves by taking the central line to North Acton, then heading up to West Ruislip. What had initially been a plan to visit all the western termini was now looking to become a desperate attempt to reach the west, one way or another! West Acton was a nice station, with lovely curved benches, as Matthew demonstrated, and a big glass front. Soon, the train arrived and we headed to North Acton, to find the train already at the other platform. Matthew knew deep down the train was taunting him, and that he'd never make it, but when he reached the top of the stairs, the call became too great, and he sprinted for his life, through the whole station, and we caught the train from the other platform to West Ruislip.

This trip finished off the entire western section of the central line for us, as we headed further into Outer London than ever before, and it became greener and leafier with every stop. Along the way, James started talking to his friend from Epping, who seemed to have no clue where one might find Chinatown and tube roundels - certainly not London! Soon we were in NR territory and nearing Boris's constituency - Ruislip. We arrived at the terminus, had a brief explore of the station, taunted James's friend from Epping with more random pictures of London (without context), including a picture of the dot-matrix, detailing a long list of trains all headed straight to Epping. We walked over to the NR platforms, and looked down the track at the wide open green fields that awaited us at the edge of London - Matthew's expedition now became feverish - we HAD to reach the edge! Thus, we started a "short walk" to find the very edge of London!

We turned left out of the station, and walked down into Ickenham (via Tesco for James to buy water to clean his soot-encrusted throat - his country lungs can't deal with the city air), and passed the lovely village pub and posh village centre. Were it not for the TfL roundels, we could have been literally anywhere in the home counties - it didn't even feel like London anymore! You could feel the Toryness eminating from every building: if there ever was a place called Middlesex, we had sure as hell reached it. It was lovely. We turned right at the village centre, and walked up the gentle slope for a "5 minute walk" to the edge.

Soon enough though, Matthew's "5 minute walk" had turned into an almost 20 minute walk, and was stretching on far too long, so after passing a house with giant TfL lettering outside, we had to turn back, despite the edge of London being merely 200 yards ahead of us, and visible for the first time, crushing Matthew's final plan. To Matthew's great upset, he decided that we should turn back, breaking his heart. We then briskly walked back to the tube station, in the hope of making James's train: it was going to be tight!

As we reached the station, with James struggling under the weight of his bag, the train we wanted pulled out of the station before our very eyes, forcing us to wait for the next one. Fortunately, the Central line, alone out of all the lines, which had decided to break now that James had brought the Curse of Kernow with him, performed excellently, and barrelled down the line in no time at all, talking about Ted Cruz, Maggie, and how long the "short walk" to PAD was going to be. We spent much of the journey down enjoying the sweet nectar of NF and arguing with TimeBot:

We passed North Acton, dived underground at White City, then made the executive decision that we didn't trust the circle line, so would chance the walk from Lancaster Gate. We arrived in Lancaster Gate on time... with about 5 minutes to go. We were operating on Matthew TimeTM now! Matthew grabbed James's bag, and started legging it!

We legged it to the lift, rose to the surface, and then ran for 3 minutes flat to Paddington. Why the fuck do we do this to ourselves? Why does every adventure end in an unpleasant sprint for a train, when we are both already dead? We sprinted across 2 dual carriageways and along a long street to the new Crossrail station entrance, and James thought we'd missed the train. We were both absolutely destroyed by the run, but we had in fact made it with 2 minutes to spare! We dashed inside, James grabbed his bag, passed through the ticket barrier, and boarded his train... except his seat was on the other end, and he couldn't get to it. Bugger. Instead, he settled for a first class seat in the back half of the train, and had a 6 hour journey home, doing Chinese, video-editing, and watching anti-lockdown protestors being beaten up by the crackheads of Kernow for crack money.

When James arrived into Plymouth, he had a problem (and that problem is transport, it take very very long as Kernow is big. Throw First Kernow, so my country can be free, so my county can be free. We shall travel nationalised and have big party.). He was quietly working through the edit and then an ear piercing noise errupted from the old man with a cane infront of him. He was sitting at a table with a stack of old, crumpled newspaper (from the 80s), three books that looked as if they had been chucked to the floor and stood on and two GWR paper cups. Now, usually in these cups, you hold tea, coffee, or maybe even apple and orange juice. However, this man had hot water in them, not just hot water but hot water infused with hole eggs. He was trying to cook eggs in GWR cups on a train with just hot water. Mmmmmmmm, yummy, food poisoning.

MY EGGGGSSSS, YOU'VE TAKEN MY EEGGGGGGSSS

The fuck? This has to the worst crackheadegg, James has come across and they lived in Camborne! We pulled up to Plymouth and the man collected his stuff and got off the train and then decided that he was going to propel himself out of the door as it was closing. After flying through the air, the man slumped onto the floor like a sack of old potatoes. After getting back to his feet, and after the train had started to leave, he starting on the train, proclaiming his eggs were the work of god and he needed them in order to live. The train pulled off as he slammed the side of the train.
