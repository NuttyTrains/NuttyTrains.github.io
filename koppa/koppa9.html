Matthew and James went on a 'little' adventure, which ended up lasting 2 days and allowed us to tick off most things from the NT wishlist. The cameras never went away, so watch out 2026 - this video will surprise you!

Despite having promised you twice that the Summer of NT was over, after Digamma 2, we thought that we were finally done. Complete. Nothing until Christmas. Big RIP, but oh well, we'd had a good run.

But then James told Matthew that he was fed up of living at home in Markeyland, and that before he returned to Cornwall, he was going to come to London for a very short Koppa adventure, and also head to Oxford to give a talk. When I came to start writing this, I realised that the rabbithole of planning this adventure was easily longer than the adventure itself, and this is not a rabbit hole I wish to fall down - it involves planes to Newquay, a quick trip to Aberdeen, and acquiring James a passport. It is the kind of plan you get when you feed Stephen some cwac.

With a few days to go, however, we had kind of hammered out a nuts plan. This is what the next train page looked like with about 1 week to go:

Welcome to the Koppa IX preparation page, here are the three things we want to do

  3. NLE
  4. Brompton / Boris Bikes

That's right - we were going to have a second crack at the DLR Challenge, finish* the tube, see the NLE, get a Brompton bike, and go to Oxford. That is easily all possible in a single day, right?

Unlike every previous time he had been in this Premier Inn, this time, he was given a new room on one of the top floors, finally separating him from his usual room. Big Sad. Once James had dumped all his worldly possessions in his room, we made our plans and set off - Genbatte!

The first port of call was PAD once more. Not for trains though, this time we were going on two wheels - it was Brompton time! We went to the special Brompton bike locker in Paddington Central, and for 20 long minutes, James fought and fought and fought with the machine. He punched in random codes, and it beeped angrily at him. He made attempt after attempt to connect to the internet, but Bowis said no. He tried his hardest to download the Brompton app, but his phone, adamant that he had not left Cornwall, refused to offer anything but Cornwall download speeds. Once downloaded, the app shoved half a tree's worth of legal documents in his face, and made him sign away his privacy, location, soul and credit card details. But when they wanted to mug him for every last penny he had for the pleasure of a single trip, that was the final straw. Fuck the Brompton bikes, they weren't worth it (renting, that is. Obviously buying one was fine).

Instead, it was back to the trains. We took the H&C to King's Cross St Pancras, and changed to the Piccadilly, winding down endless corridors, getting lost, and fighting the occasional minotaur. Nice to see that Scott has found himself a new job.

We zoomed straight up the line, all the way to Cockfosters at the end, eventually surfacing near Southgate. The train pulled into Cockfosters, our ante-penultimate tube terminus, whereupon we had our ceremonial picture and set about our latest task: it was Charles Holden time!

The next 2-ish hours were dominated by James being a full-on anorak. Matthew had never seen anything like it. What monster had he created? Whilst he was perfectly content to sit in the station garden (which was very nice), James was filming anything that moved (and plenty that didn't), including all the trains, the magnificent station roof, the wide concourse, the 2 main entrances, the ex-ticket offices, the new step-free lift, and all the passengers who came too close to him. We surfaced, had a brief walk over to the start of the GREEN, filmed some shit in the lift, and Matthew ran off with the camera. All in a day's work really. Cockfosters was our joint-favourite station on this end section of line, but before long we were back on the train to our next stop: Oakwood.

Oakwood station also didn't disappoint, and unlike Oakwood in Pembrokeshire, no-one died here. On the station list, we called this, our third-favourite station, "Box", and that sums it up pretty well - it was a box. No questions asked. We saw the cool roundel/lamppost in the car park, and moved onto the crown jewel...

Southgate, also a joint-favourite, did not disappoint. It was magnificent. The flying-saucer shaped station was everything Matthew had promised, and more. It is easy to see why this is often called the best station on the tube. It was a shame that we couldn't get any cool long-exposure shots at night, but hopefully our long-exposure day shots and shitty clips make up for it. We even had a meal deal try to follow us onto the road here!

Finally, we reached Arnos Grove and Bounds Green, cyclindrically- and octagonally-shaped respectively. These were also cool Charles Holden stations, but they were our least favourite of the 5 (not that that is harsh criticism). We finished off our segment here by filming some segment at the top of a random bridge, after we both had the same idea at the same time (which turned out to be a rubbish shot). Illi Vitae. It was now time to head south and try and do some other shit before the sun set. Genbatte!

We threw some ideas back and forth, but eventually Matthew decided to ignore all input from James and just execute the first plan that came into his head without giving James any warning about when or where we were changing trains. We changed twice in rapid succession at Finsbury Park and Highbury & Islington, and eventually found our way onto the slowverground. That's right folks, we might be almost finished with the tube, but we have plenty more slowverground to cover!

Stop moaning, delicious content is delicious content!

We waited for ages at Highbury & Islington for our correct train after we missed the first by a few seconds (the second decided to be especially slow just to rub salt into an open wound), before finally heading down the East London Line into Art Student HQ. James might think he has it bad in Falmouth, but one look at Shoreditch, even at a social distance up upon a viaduct, left scarred him mentally for life. That was bad, and he vowed never to go there again!

We also passed through Whitechapel on this line, but unfortunately saw no purp evidence :( Whitechapel is the only location in London where the underground passes over the overground and the overground passes under the underground. This fried James' dyspraxic brain and he wanted to leave.

At Canada Water, Matthew handed over control of the adventure to James' stomach, which was now on a 15-minute countdown to genocidal fury, the kind of anger felt only by a drunk, high "inhabitant" of Townhill when Markey tried to fob them off by replacing their daily cwac benefits with weed benefits. You saw what they did when they burned those cars for a laugh! James' stomach was about to do the same unless we found food NOW!

We headed to London Bridge and then walked towards the river, past the Shard and Tower Bridge, down through some Victorian warehouses, and into Pizza Express. James spent the entire walk snapping photos of the Shard from all kinds of angles, before turning around and saying that he didn't understand the point of skyscrapers, and that they should knock them all down, and replace them with a library, some fields, some fishing boats and a Bargain Booze, just like they have down Camborne. Before promptly turning around and snapping some more pictures.

"I don't get the point of Tower Bridge. It is just a bridge" - James, K9, after killing his camera battery with endless Tower Bridge shots on K8

We caught a train to Charing Cross, being some of the only people left on the train after it passed Waterloo East, and added another London terminus to our growing collection. We passed the very centre of London (Charing Cross) and walked for about 5 minutes to Covent Garden, where we split ways, and James met up with Molly for what would be a very fun night...

James spent his evening getting blind drunk when seeing Molly, got his bag searched in a club, and got touched up by a woman. All in a day's work really.
