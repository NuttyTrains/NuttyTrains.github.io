I am not going to step on James' toes here, given that this is the largest chunk of text James has ever written, or will ever write on this website. So I am briefly going to expand upon my (now-limited) memories of this adventure, 6 months after it happened.

This adventure was born from the ashes of NT Epsilon, which was cancelled thanks to Boris. This adventure would have involved Matthew catching a dirt-cheap early morning train to Exeter (SWR *vomits*), before we slowly headed east to the Isle of Wight. We cancelled all our tickets, afraid of chockdown 3, but with only days to go, with chockdown 3 not happening (yet), Matthew still had his dirt-cheap ticket to Exeter. Exmouth Line

The first port of call was the Exmouth line. We reversed back up the line towards Salisbury, before turning towards the coast. We passed through a number of quiet stations, and the line ran alongside the muddy flats of the Exe floodplain. James gave Matthew an ornithology lesson, by teaching him the names of all the lines in the South West, all named after birds that Matthew had never heard of or seen before. Nor can he remember them, because this was 6 months ago. All he can remember is that we spotted some bird-like things by the river. It was a yummy grey December day, so the river and the sky looked beautiful.

As far as South West branch lines go, this was the easiest of the bunch, despite Matthew thinking it was extremely complicated. Once per hour, a train would stop at every station on the line, with only 2 request stops. Unfortunately, the latter of these, Lympstone Commando, was completely dead, with almost 0 passengers. James assumed this would be the main difficulty.

Fortunately, someone wanted to stop at Exton, the other request stop, so that left only Lympstone Commando. We asked the conductor if he could stop the train. As we pulled into the station, we were greeted by a horrible concrete and barbed wire monstrosity of an army base. It looked like Colditz in the grey mizzle. We both hopped off, and James started to walk towards the exit, fully intending to walk to Lympstone Village. Matthew took another look at Colditz, looked back at the train, jumped back on, and said "thank you" to the conductor, before sitting down, to James' horror. The audacity of that boy! The cheek! The nerve! Down Kernow, you'd never do that! That damn city boy!

We rocked up into "sunny", "pleasant" Exmouth - a vile port town surrounded by a grey pallid sea and beige mudflats. We fucked around for a little, Matthew walked backwards through a tunnel, we jumped over a fence, got bored, had our station photo, and returned to the train, this time heading to Torquay.

Torquay

We headed back up to Exeter, and then passed straight through, heading down the Devon mainline towards Kernow. As tempted as James was to make the trip to Kernow itself, he decided that today would be focussed on Torquay and Paignton. We'd both recently watched Fawlty Towers - maybe that clouded our judgement.

We travelled down the mainline, this time not running alongside the river, but alongside the sea; the freezing, pallid, grey waves crashing against the railway embankment. Apart from the imminent premonitions of death as we passed through Dawlish, this was actually a nice section of line. Lots of pretty seaside villages. Having the line so close to the sea would have been beautiful in summer... why did we choose to do this in December?

Editor's Note: James is never in Kernow in the summer anymore. That is why

Before long, we were in Newton Abbott, and turned off the mainline to head to Torquay and Paignton. It was Basil Fawlty time.

James relives his childhood

Torquay station was actually very nice. A nice old building. That is probably what enticed us off the train in the first place, instead of continuing on to Paignton. When we realised we were now stuck here for almost an hour, we decided to do some exploring. We headed down to the promenade, and walked alongside the beach, the turgid, bleached sand, the turbulent, foaming sea, and the grey, mizzling sky. Beautiful scenes from NT. It was postcard-perfect.

Along we went, passing the handful of tourists questioning why they were there in December, and walked towards the town. Christ, it was some distance. Clearly somebody wanted to save money (*cough*, GWR, *cough*). We arrived in the town's marina, and found literally everything to be closed. The cafe was basically closed, the shops were closed, but worst of all...

Depression. Living Coasts is ded.

Living Coasts was ded. That central feature of James' childhood had been chocked out of existence. RIP. James regaled Matthew with many fun stories about birds becoming stuck in the netting above the zoo and dying/being eaten by seagulls.

Matthew amused himself by wandering around the multi-storey car park. It wasn't huge, but it scored highly for dinginess, dripping pipes, sodium lighting, and being attached to an abandoned, decaying aquarium. After we both managed to get a bit lost, we decided that the best method of suicide prevention for James (Living Coasts was ded) would be to return to the town. So that's what we did.

MOAARR Torquay

We made our way through the ocean of boarded-up stores, tacky gift shops, and high street chains that are allegedly the envy of the South West (or so said James' mum when she discovered that Torquay was the location of that rarest of sights - a Primark). We walked through the marina and up the high street. It had been more than 10 minutes since Matthew's last coffee, so he was on the verge of complete bodily shutdown. After passing such luxury retail offerings as Poundland, Shoe Zone and Greggs (side note - I just went on Google Maps to check this - I had completely forgotten about "License to Fill", the sandwich shop. How could I forget that?), we went into Caffe Nero to re-caffinate Matthew.

We were pretty much done with Torquay by this point, so decided to head slowly back to the station. Matthew, for some unknown reason, was in charge of navigating, and he decided that the best way of doing this was to head straight up a cliff, over a headland, and straight down the cliff on the other end. Up and up we walked, winding our way up the cliff-face, with neither of us knowing where we were going. At some point, Matthew must have realised his mistake, because we descended into yet another multi-storey car park, and raced each other to the ground. Unfortunately, once again, we didn't bother to set an end point, and so managed to completely miss each other on the way down. Great success!

As we made our way around the bay, back to the station, we soon realised that we were simply not going to make this train. In true Matthew fashion, he suggested we walk to Paignton. However, as this was James' adventure, he decided that we should instead head to the beach to kill an hour. The beach. Matthew's favourite place.

James and Matthew were attacked by some angry swans as we walked over to the edge of the beach, to a large sign emblazoned "THESE ROCKS ARE CUT OFF BY INCOMING TIDE". Perfect. Exactly what we wanted. Matthew was also attacked by this strange turbulent fluid that continued to oscilate its way towards him, menacingly. James called it "the sea".
